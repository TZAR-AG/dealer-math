# Lead Magnet — Give vs Withhold (2026-05-01 strategic brief)

> **Purpose.** Inform the Hormozi+Martell-style interview that refines the AU Dealer Math "7 Lines on a Dealer Contract" PDF. Prepared for Adrian. Written as a strategy session, not a textbook.
>
> **TL;DR.** The current 7-Lines PDF is *under-giving on emotion, over-giving on diagnostics, and under-pulling on the next problem.* It teaches a buyer how to read a contract — but it doesn't show the buyer the world they're in, and it doesn't open a loop that only Macca's videos (and the eventual paid product) can close. Two specific clauses need to come out (or be transformed) and one missing element (the "next problem") needs to go in.

---

## 1. Headline philosophy

A B2C "insider truth" lead magnet has one job: **make the reader feel they just stepped behind a curtain they didn't know existed, then leave them desperate to step further.** Not "here is a checklist." More like "here is the corner of the map you didn't know was redacted." Hormozi's narrow-problem rule and Martell's 5-10 principle agree on the mechanism — solve one specific painful moment completely, reveal the larger system that contains it, and route the next problem to your channel and your eventual paid product.

The current 7-Lines PDF is doing what Adrian flagged: it's a competent contract-decoder. That's a *diagnostic* tool, not a *worldview* tool. Diagnostics are commoditised — every state consumer-affairs department in Australia publishes a contract-decoder. Macca's moat isn't "what to look for in a contract." It's **the choreography behind the contract** — the holdback, the changeover misdirection, the three-room trap, the manager lie, the rate range game. The PDF should give the reader enough of that worldview to make them *suspicious for life*, then tell them every Mon/Wed/Fri at 6pm AWST a specific lever gets pulled apart.

**The reframe:** stop thinking of the PDF as "a contract guide." Start thinking of it as "the moment Macca shows you the seam in the matrix." One seam, fully shown. The seam pulls the rest of the curtain.

## 2. The give/withhold framework

Hormozi's framing is the load-bearing one for AUDM: *give the WHAT, charge for the HOW.* Restated for a free B2C PDF:

| Give freely (in PDF) | Tighten (give 80% in PDF, 20% gated) | Withhold (video/paid only) |
|---|---|---|
| **Frameworks + naming** ("the Three-Room Trap", "the Manager Lie", "the Changeover Misdirection"). Naming is brand glue. The viewer/reader memorising the *name* of a move is what makes them recognise it on the showroom floor — and what makes them quote Macca to a mate. Give every name. | **One specific dollar figure per clause as illustrative anchor** (e.g. "dealer delivery is typically $1,500-$3,000"). One range, qualified, stays in the PDF. Specifics scaling with brand stay in video. | **Exact word tracks** the salesperson uses. "Where's the cash from? Savings? HELOC? Previous loan?" That's a Macca-on-camera reveal — high-trust, hard-to-forget. Burn that on YouTube where the algo rewards the viral specificity. The PDF can name the *category* of move ("the Cash-to-Finance Interrogation") without giving the script. |
| **Behind-the-curtain stories** ("the manager sat in the other office calculating margin while I walked back and forth"). Stories build the persona, the persona builds the brand, the brand drives subs + future paid. Stories are the cheapest moat to give and the hardest to copy. | **One actionable script per clause** (the "fix" line). One reader takeaway per page is the right density — more is overwhelming, less is forgettable. | **Brand-specific commission percentages, holdback ranges, agency-fee figures, manufacturer rebate splits.** These are video content. Anchored on public-record where possible (Mercedes Federal Court precedent, AADA carsales reporting), spoken not written, and only on V11 where the brand-naming policy permits. |
| **The "what's hidden" reveal** — one per clause. The whole PDF's value is the reader saying "no one told me that." Withholding the hidden mechanic defeats the point. | **The reframe / the principle behind the move** (e.g. "the customer feels they won, but the dealership reached a number they were always willing to do"). Give the principle in PDF, give the *demonstration in real time* in video. | **Specific salesperson body language / pen/sigh/wait calibration choreography** (Q1/Q2 deal-50 material). That's your highest-engagement video gold — it can't be written without losing 70% of its impact. |
| **One simple consumer hack per topic** that can be applied tomorrow — the "fix" line. This is the Quick Win (Deiss). Without it, the PDF is just a complaint. | | **Worked-math examples with stacked layers** (the Camry trade-in math with $14,990 retail / $10K offer / $1K recon / $4K trade gross). One in-PDF example is fine. The rest belong in video where Macca can talk through them and the algo rewards the watch time. |

### The two principles that underpin the table

1. **Hormozi's narrow problem rule.** A great free magnet solves *one* meaningful problem fully, then *reveals* the next problem the reader didn't know they had — and only Macca's videos (and eventual paid product) close that loop. Salty pretzels make you thirsty. Right now the 7-Lines PDF is *seven half-eaten pretzels*. It teaches the reader to look at seven lines, but doesn't make them thirsty for the choreography that produced those lines.
2. **Martell's 5-10 principle.** A good lead magnet takes ~5 minutes to consume and delivers a result they could implement in 10 minutes (or less, in our case — the result is "you walk into a dealership and recognise the move"). Eight pages of dense contract clauses overshoots the consumption budget. The reader skims, gets the vibe, doesn't internalise. The fix isn't more pages — it's tighter pages with one move per page that *sticks*.

### The 10% rule, calibrated

Hormozi: lead magnet = 10% of the buyer's problem solved, awareness of the other 90% created. For a future Australian who'll spend $30K-$70K on a car within 12 months, "the other 90%" is the live-match arena: actually negotiating against an F&I manager who's run 8,000 deals. The PDF can't substitute for the muscle memory. It can give the reader the playbook — but the rep happens against another human, in real time, on a Tuesday. Macca's videos build the reps. The future paid product (when it arrives) is the simulator — script libraries, calculator tools, deal-by-deal walkthroughs, maybe a deal-coach service. The PDF gives the reader enough to *want* the simulator.

## 4. AUDM-specific: each of the 7 current clauses

Format: **CLAUSE — verdict — why — what to change.**

### Line 1: Drive-away price / dealer delivery
**Verdict: KEEP, sharpen.**
Strongest of the seven. The "dealer delivery is markup, not a fee" reveal is brand-true (Macca's voice, Honda-era data) and the "ask for the ex-on-road price" fix is genuinely actionable. *Sharpen:* tighten the dollar range to "$1,500-$3,000 in our experience" with the AU caveat. Add a one-line "this is one of the smaller asymmetries in the deal — the bigger ones are coming."

### Line 2: Trade-in value "as inspected"
**Verdict: KEEP, expand the next-problem hook.**
The "dealers reassess and reduce on settlement" is true and worth giving. But it's also *only the surface mechanic* — the actual play is the **Walk-Around Doubt-Plant** (#22 in the bank) + **Manager Lie / Two-Step Margin Hold** (#24) + **Changeover Misdirection** (#25). *Change:* keep the line, then close it with one teaser sentence: *"This is the surface of a four-move sequence the dealership runs on every trade. V4 (Mon 11 May) breaks the whole thing down."* That's the next-problem reveal.

### Line 3: Finance through dealer panel rate
**Verdict: TIGHTEN.**
"Dealer earns 1-3% origination commission" is approximately right but the real story is the **Rate Range Game** (#41) — start-rate vs floor-rate calibrated to customer pushback. The current line states the *outcome* without the *mechanic*. *Change:* keep the framework name, soften the specific %, add one line: *"How they pick which rate to write you at is its own choreography — V2 (Wed 6 May) is the Finance Manager's Office walkthrough."* Hooks straight to V2 which is the next-published video.

### Line 4: Loan protection insurance
**Verdict: KEEP, name the room.**
"50-80% commission" is fine. The reveal that strengthens this clause is naming **the Aftercare Room** (#34, #35) and the fact that 60-80% of customers get attached. *Change:* "dealer commission" → "this is one product in The Aftercare Room — typically 60-80% of buyers leave with at least one of these. V3 (Fri 8 May) is a top-to-bottom walkthrough." Three videos hooked in three lines now.

### Line 5: 14-day vehicle-ready clause
**Verdict: REMOVE or REPLACE.**
This is the weakest. *Why:* (a) it's a niche edge case — most readers will never hit a 14-day price-drop scenario; (b) the "fix" (renegotiate clause to reprice on market drop) is unrealistic — no AU dealer will sign that addendum; (c) it doesn't connect to a flagship video; (d) it dilutes the seven-line density with a low-value entry.
*Replacement candidate (strongest):* **"Estimated payout figure" / negative equity (V5, Wed 13 May).** Real, common, financially catastrophic, has a flagship video already locked. The clause is the *Estimated Payout line* on the contract — bank issues real payout post-signature, customer's weekly payment shifts. Hooks V5 perfectly.

### Line 6: Pre-delivery inspection
**Verdict: TIGHTEN, depersonalise the stat.**
"~30% of PDIs are tick-box" is the kind of specific number that creates defamation surface in writing. *Why:* in spoken video Macca can say "in my experience" + the line lands as personal testimony. In a published PDF, that 30% reads as a factual claim about the AU industry — defensible, but uncomfortable. *Change:* replace with "in our experience PDI thoroughness varies dramatically by dealership and brand." The fix (independent $200 inspection) is gold — keep that. Add: *"V12 territory — what your delivery handover should and shouldn't include."*

### Line 7: Acknowledgement clause
**Verdict: REMOVE.**
*Why:* (a) it's legalistic and unactionable — the "fix" (cross out things, initial them) does almost nothing for the buyer in practice and may be unenforceable; (b) it doesn't map to any framework in the bank; (c) it's the weakest takeaway-per-page in the PDF; (d) it ends the PDF on a flat note instead of a hook.
*Replacement candidate (strongest):* **"Where the salesperson hands you off to the next room"** — the **Signposting Handoff** (#37). This is *the* moment the buyer can either tap out or get walked through three rooms of margin extraction. The reveal: the handoff line itself is calibrated to keep you compliant. The fix: ask for time between rooms, decline products on the spot, refuse to let the F&I manager re-open the metal price. Closes the PDF on the moment of maximum buyer leverage and previews the back half of the channel content.

## 6. The 5 most important questions Hormozi+Martell would ask Adrian

Frame for the interview. These are the questions that, when answered, will either lock the PDF v2 design or expose the missing pieces.

1. **"What is the *one* moment in a dealership transaction where, if the buyer remembered one thing from your PDF, they would save the most money?"** — Forces Adrian to rank. Hormozi's narrow-problem test. If the answer is "the changeover misdirection" or "the F&I rate range" — that's the *anchor* of the PDF, not seven equal-weight lines. The answer probably reveals a different structure: one big idea, supported by six tactical sub-points, instead of seven peer-level points.

2. **"If you sold a $29 product tomorrow, what would the buyer get that this PDF doesn't have?"** — Forces Adrian to articulate the moat. Hormozi: the PDF must reveal the *next* problem. If Adrian can't name what the $29 product would deliver that the PDF doesn't, the PDF is over-giving (no upsell exists) or the paid product hasn't been thought through (no value gap to bridge). The answer probably is: scripts library + decision-tree calculator + per-state stamp duty/LCT calculator + deal-by-deal walkthroughs. That's the gated layer.

3. **"What's the most damaging specific dollar figure or word-track you could put in writing, and what happens if you do?"** — The defamation calibration question. Forces Adrian to consciously choose what stays verbal-only. Mercedes 4.7% agent fee is the canonical example — public-record range exists, but the inside-source figure stays out of any written PDF. Same logic applies to "30% of PDIs are tick-box" — defensible spoken, exposed written.

4. **"If a buyer reads this PDF and never watches a video, did you do your job?"** — Hormozi's give-the-WHAT test. If Adrian says "yes" — the PDF is a substitute for the channel and the funnel is broken. If he says "no, the PDF is the appetiser and they need the choreography in motion to actually beat the manager" — that's correct, and it tells you the PDF must be *unsatisfying without the channel* by design. The current 7-Lines PDF is satisfying enough that some readers will never click through.

5. **"What's the line in your real career that you would never tell a stranger in a coffee shop, but you'd tell a $29 customer?"** — The moat sentry. Identifies what's high-value, brand-true, story-grade, and *paywall-worthy*. Probably it's a specific manager-level move he learned at BMW or Mercedes that he won't tell on a free channel. That sentence belongs in the eventual paid product as an opening anchor — "this is what you bought."

## 7. Risks / red flags / "don't do this"

- **Don't add a calculator inside the PDF.** Calculators are decision tools. Decision tools are the eventual paid product. Free PDF + free calculator + free script library = no upsell wedge. Keep the PDF informational; build calculators as separate gated assets later.

- **Don't go below 7 pages.** Buyers are about to spend $30K-$70K — they want to feel they got something substantial. A 3-page PDF reads disposable for this purchase size. Martell's 5-minute consume rule still permits 8-12 pages of dense single-spaced content.

- **Don't write scripts verbatim.** "Where's the cash from? Savings? HELOC? Previous loan?" reads as defamation-adjacent in writing — lifted from a real F&I training. In video, it's testimony from Macca's persona. In a PDF, it's "AUDM published an internal F&I training script." Not a hill we want to die on. Name the move; don't transcribe the script.

- **Don't mention specific dealership names, brand commission %s, or specific manufacturer rebate dollar figures in the PDF.** The brand-naming policy applies harder in writing than in video. PDF stays category-level. Mercedes-by-name only on V11 *video*, never in PDF.

- **Don't pad with "tips from a former dealer" boilerplate.** The reveal is the persona's *specific* take, not generic dealership-survival advice. If a sentence could appear on any state consumer-affairs page, cut it.

- **Don't use stock photography of cars / handshakes / contracts.** Document-forensics aesthetic per the design system. Top-down shots, cream paper, charcoal tone, orange highlight on one line per page. The PDF's *look* must match the channel's look — the brand artefact lock matters more than any single sentence.

- **Don't position the PDF as a "checklist."** Checklists die fast. Position it as *"the seven moments Macca learned to recognise"* — testimony framing, not utility framing. Lasts longer in the reader's head, more screenshot-shareable, less competitive with state-government guides.

- **Don't end on "subscribe to AU Dealer Math." Generic.** End on "*V2 publishes Wed 6 May at 6pm AWST. The Finance Manager's Office. The five-line script every F&I manager runs on every customer who said the word 'cash'.*" Specific cliffhanger > generic CTA. Hormozi's lead-magnet cardinal rule: every magnet ends with one specific next door.

- **Don't refresh the PDF every video drop.** Cadence creep. Pick a stable v2 design, lock it for a quarter, re-score against quiz-completion / channel-sub conversion data after V12 lands.

- **Don't move the PDF behind a quiz wall (yet).** The Structured Self quiz funnel is the eventual analogue, but at AUDM cold-start the email-for-PDF trade is the right friction. Re-score at 1K subs.
