### Origin

- **Background pre-dealer:** refrigeration apprenticeship
- **Entry trigger:** friend's brother worked at BMW, drove a brand new BMW home every time. Adrian saw it and decided he wanted that lifestyle.
- **The interview:** walked in late afternoon after a refrigeration shift. No appointment. Wore pointy shoes + white socks + trousers + white shirt. 3-hour interview with sales manager + assistant SM. Hit with the "sell me this pen" Wolf of Wall Street test — Adrian stumbled, couldn't even remember the movie. Hired anyway.
- **Manager's mentorship:** introduced Adrian to Simon Sinek, Gary Vaynerchuk ("Co and Ray"), Seth Godin, and NLP (neuro-linguistic programming). Manager was deeper than typical — talked life + mentorship, not just sales.
- **Onboarding doc:** "Road to a Sale" — the basic sales process every new salesperson learned. Apply to walk-in customers.

### First deal — Janet, Honda HRV Morpheus Blue

- **When:** Wednesday night, first week.
- **The car:** Honda HRV in Morpheus Blue.
- **The customer:** Janet.
- **The math:** approximately $20K asking → Janet offered $18K → multiple trips → settled around $19.5K.
- **Adrian's calibration:** ZERO. When the manager said *"go back and get more money"*, Adrian thought it would be quite difficult. He didn't know what the normal was.
- **The manager's script:** *"Just tell her we can't do this. We can do 19 and a half."* Adrian crossed out the number on the contract and walked back to Janet.
- **The trips:** 2-3 round trips, dropping $100s at a time.
- **What the manager was doing:** sitting in the other office. Checking how cheap they could sell the car for, calculating dealership margin + Adrian's commission.
- **The outcome:** Janet eventually agreed. She "felt like she had a bit of a win."

### The first lesson Adrian can't unlearn

> "She felt like she had a bit of a win."

This is the central insight. The dealership got to a number they were always willing to do. Janet didn't actually win — she just felt like she did. The car is the delivery mechanism. The FEELING of winning is the product.

The "theatre" — going backwards and forwards, the manager visible in the other office, the contract being crossed out and rewritten — is the choreography that manufactures the buyer's win-feeling.

### Q1 asked
*"Take me to one deal you remember sharply — not Janet's, somewhere around deal 50 or 100. The buyer drove out certain they'd beaten you. Walk me through the last 20 minutes — manager position, pen behaviour, wait-time, your face when walking back, the buyer's signing tell."*

### Adrian's answer — verbatim choreography of the dialled-in close

**Step 1 — Color-narrowing scarcity manufacture.**
> "What sort of colours are you guys leaning towards? Maybe white, maybe black — okay, let's narrow it to one colour."
Then look at the computer: *"Great news, we've got one left in white."* Even though there might be 10 white cars on the lot.

**Step 2 — Type the contract without permission.**
Start typing the offer-to-purchase before they've agreed to anything. When they ask what you're doing: *"Just putting the information on the offer-to-purchase so I can show you all the inclusions. Then you can make a decision today."* They say *"oh, okay"* and you're already drafting.

**Step 3 — The upside-down read.**
Put the contract in front of them. Read it upside down — line by line — so they're reading it right-way-up:
- Name, email, phone — *"yep"*
- Address — *"yep"*
- Car details, inclusions — *"yep"*
- Driveaway price — *"yep"*
- *"And if you're happy with everything, all I need is a $1,000 deposit today and a signature here. Then I'll take it to my manager and get it signed off."*

**Step 4 — The Four-Door objection isolator.**
They say *"oh no, we're not signing today, still got a few cars to look at."*
> *"Okay — what's stopping you from making a decision today? Have we missed something? Are you going to look at other cars? Is it the price? Is it me?"*
Forces them to name the actual blocker.

**Step 5 — The Hard No First (the move I graduated to).**
Beginner version: customer says $17K, salesperson crosses out $20K, writes $17K, walks to manager. Manager bounces back. Repeat.
Advanced version: customer says $17K → *"That's obviously not possible. It has to be $20,000."* Strong no, hold the line. Push them to the point where they're about to walk. THEN soften: *"Look, I'll try my best to close the gap from what you want to pay to what we want to sell the car for."*

**Step 6 — Close the gap before writing anything down.**
Get them up verbally — $17K → $18,500 — *before* the pen touches the contract. Only when the gap is closed as far as it'll go does the pen come out.

**Step 7 — Sign before I walk back.**
Cross out the driveaway price. Write their offer ($18,500) on the contract. Get them to **initial and sign** the contract right there. Now they're committed. *"They've worn me out based on what they want to pay. I've done my job. I already know this is a deal."*

**Step 8 — "Wish me luck."**
Before walking to the manager: *"Alright, wish me luck. I'll try my best for you to get over the line."* Builds hope and flips the frame — now we're on the same team against the manager.

**Step 9 — Let me try one more time + micro-concession close.**
Go to the manager — who already knows the deal because Adrian's been in communication the whole time. Manager: *"Can you get more?"* Adrian: *"Yes."*
Walk back: *"Look, unfortunately I can't do $18,500. I can get it done for $19."* Pen ready to write 19.
Customer hesitates, wants to leave. *"Let me try one more time."* Cross out again. *"Can I try $18,750?"*
Back to the manager. Manager signs off. Adrian goes back: *"Look, I can get it done for you at $18,750 — and let's add free floor mats on top."*

Manager signs. Customer leaves with what feels like a hard-won deal + a bonus.

The bonus stack appears to be a universal premium-brand mechanic, not a brand-specific one.

### Q5 ASKED + ANSWERED 2026-04-29 — The trade-in spread

**The full trade-in choreography:**

1. **Sign-posted at qualifying.** *"Do you have a trade-in?"* asked early. *"What I'll do next, come and tell me a little bit about your car. We'll go around it, fill out all the information. My sales manager / valuer will look at the vehicle while we go for a test drive. When we're back, if you're happy with the trading price my manager gives me, we'll look at making a deal today."* — sets the urgency frame + pre-commits the customer to deciding today.

2. **The "I don't value it" distancing.** Salesperson explicitly disclaims valuation responsibility — sets up the manager as neutral arbiter, salesperson as ally.

3. **The walk-around doubt-plant.** Salesperson goes around the trade WITH the customer:
    - Touches scratches while pointing
    - *"How long have you had it?"* / *"Any known issues?"*
    - *"What happened here?"* (gets the customer confessing defects)
    - *"How much did it cost you last time to change the tires?"*
    - *"How much do you reckon this will cost to fix?"*
    - Asks *"do you have any idea how much you want for it?"* — reads customer confidence (some lie high, some low, some refuse)
   - Customer leaves walk-around pre-conditioned for a low offer.

4. **The valuation while-test-drive.** Customer + salesperson go for test drive. Sales manager / valuer takes keys, looks at the market. *"If we bought this car, what can we sell it for?"* Time pressure: dealership wants to flick used stock in **1-2 weeks** — overhang is opex.

5. **The recon allocation.** Every used car ingested gets **$1,000 auto-allocated for reconditioning**. Unused → flows to used-car-recon pool on the P&L. Exceeded (e.g. $2K engine fix) → margin squeeze but still buffered.

6. **The Manager Lie / Two-Step Margin Hold.** Manager values trade internally at the maximum (~$10K on the Camry example). Tells salesperson a lower number ($9K). Salesperson delivers $9K to customer. Customer pushes back. Manager "graciously" releases $500-1K of held room. **The salesperson is lied to by design** — their authentic frustration with the customer's pushback is what makes the customer believe the salesperson is on their side.

**Worked example — 2018 Camry 95,000km trade in toward $35K new:**

| Layer | $ |
|---|---|
| Retail target on lot | $14,990 |
| Trade offer to customer | $10,000 (or $9,500 after manager "wins" them $500) |
| Built-in recon allocation | −$1,000 |
| **Trade-in front gross** | **~$4,000** |
| Recon allocation if unused | flows to pool (+~$1K avg uplift) |

**Critical content insight:** A trade-in customer is negotiating **two stacked deals** simultaneously — the new car AND the used car — and only has visibility on the new one. The dealer's TOTAL gross is roughly: new-car front ($2-5K) + trade-in front (~$4K) + recon-pool drift + F&I + aftercare + holdback + volume rebate.

### Q5 ADDENDUM 2026-04-29 — The Changeover Misdirection + Bottom-of-Market pricing

**The Changeover Misdirection (the move that follows the trade-price objection):**
- Customer is *always* unhappy with the trade price. Dealership knows this is going to be the objection.
- Salesperson writes up the contract: *"$35,000 car − $9,000 trade = $26,000 changeover."*
- Customer baulks at the trade price.
- Salesperson **shifts the negotiation from trade-in $ → changeover $.** *"This is where the deal is — what changeover do you want?"*
- Customer says e.g. *"I'll do it if you can do $25K changeover."*
- Same Hard No First / close-the-gap / write-down / initial-and-sign mechanic from Q1, transposed onto the changeover number.
- **The customer feels they won $1K on the changeover. They didn't change the trade. They moved the new-car gross by $1K and never noticed.**

**Worked example:** $35K new − $9K trade = $26K changeover. Customer wants $25K changeover. If salesperson lands $25,500 with $9K trade held, customer leaves believing they "won the trade up to $9,500." Actually the trade stayed at $9K and the new-car gross gave up $500.

**Used-car pricing strategy (Adrian as sales manager / GM):**
- Look at carsales last 6 months — listed AND delisted comparables.
- Price ours to be at the **bottom** of that visible band.
- Goal: turn inventory in 1-2 weeks (don't bleed floorplan interest).
- Trade-off chosen: lower retail = faster turn = lower opex.

26. **Bottom-of-Market Used Pricing** — dealer lists at the floor of the carsales comparable band to turn inventory fast. Cheapest listing ≠ best deal. It's the dealer hitting a turn target.

### Q5 ADDENDUM 2 2026-04-29 — Payout, negative equity, finance fallovers

**The Estimated Payout Line and what happens when it's wrong:**
- Customer doesn't know exact payout — common case.
- Salesperson pushes hard to find out: missing number = can't close today.
- Contract written with *estimated payout* line. Customer signs based on a guess.
- Bank issues real payout letter post-signature. If it matches: math is simple, deal proceeds.
- **If estimate is catastrophically wrong (real payout much higher):** contract reopens, finance manager re-runs numbers. Customer's weekly payment shifts up (e.g. $250/wk → $300/wk).

**The Loan-to-Vehicle Ratio Wall (hard limit on negative equity):**
- Worked example: trade valued $30K, customer payout $50K, new car $70K. $20K negative equity stacked on top of new car = ~$90K total loan against a $70K asset.
- Bank rejects on loan-to-vehicle ratio.
- Customer options collapse to:
  - Big deposit (which dealer flags as "not good" — they want the customer to finance, where back-end commission lives)
  - Walk away ("wrong time to buy a new car")
  - Sell privately (which loses the deal AND loses the customer — they don't come back)
- **Major Aussie consumer reveal: deep-negative-equity customers are structurally trapped in their current car until they pay it down or sell privately.**

**The Payment-Not-Price Pivot (universal protocol — Adrian: "100% of the time"):**
- Customer financing + stuck on a number → ALWAYS pivot from total $ to weekly $.
- Customer says *"$30K changeover, I'll think about it."* Salesperson says *"but it's only $300/week."*
- Reframes the customer's mental anchor from price → payment. Most consumers can't fluently translate weekly payments back to total $ + interest cost.

**Finance Fallovers (industry-named phenomenon):**
- Customer signs → bank rejects → deal canceled.
- "Happens all the time."
- The signed contract is provisional until bank confirmation. Part of why the Estimated Payout trick works (customer committed but deal reversible).
- Also explains the dealer's hard push to close *today* — the longer the sign-to-bank-confirm gap, the more chances of fallover.

### Frameworks extracted from Q5 addendum 2 (3 new — running total: 30)

28. **The Payment-Not-Price Pivot** — universal. Reframe total → weekly. Single most reliable closer when customer is financing.
29. **The Loan-to-Vehicle Ratio Wall** — bank rejects when negative equity + new car loan exceeds ratio limit. Deep-negative-equity customers are trapped in current car.
30. **Finance Fallovers** — signed contracts are provisional until bank confirmation. Common dealership occurrence.

## Q6 ASKED + ANSWERED 2026-04-29 — The F&I + Aftercare Rooms

**Aftercare manager (sees customer FIRST after sales handoff):**
- Pitches: paint protection (interior + exterior), window tint, interior protection, dash cameras (Adrian named four; full menu also includes fabric protection, ceramic coating, tyre & rim insurance, alloy upgrades).
- **Margins (Hormozi-grade specifics):**
  - Paint protection plan: **$2,000 retail / ~$500 cost / 75% margin** ($1,500 gross per attach)
  - Window tint: **$600-700 retail / $200-300 cost / ~60% margin** ($300-500 gross per attach)
- **Penetration rate: 60-80%** (industry standard)
- *"Every dealership focuses very hard to increase these percentages."*

**Finance manager (sees customer SECOND, after aftercare):**
- **Three customer types arrive at the F&I door:**
  1. **"Cash" buyers** — F&I converts them. *"Most cash is bullshit"* — most "cash" is HELOC / mortgage redraw / remortgage. Customer ends up putting a $50K car loan over a 30-year term to chase a lower interest rate. Worse math than dealer finance.
  2. **Novated lease customers** — employer-permitted scheme bundling tax saving + servicing + tires + fuel + windscreen. Pitch: *"save $20K/year on tax."* **Lease companies never disclose the interest rate.** Most people fall for it; case-by-case whether it actually beats alternatives.
  3. **Standard finance customers** — F&I closes the loan and packages F&I products.
- **F&I manager's two levers:** fees + interest rate. Both adjustable up/down to land the deal.
  - Bank approves customer at base rate (e.g. 7%); F&I writes contract at higher rate (e.g. 9%). Term-multiplied spread is the dealer's commission.
- **Penetration rate: 20-30%** (industry standard for finance attach)

**The Three-Salesperson Sequence — confirmed identity layer on the Three-Room Trap:**
> *"Every finance manager and every aftercare manager is also a salesperson. They will have their own ways and their own word tracks to apply pressure on the customer."*
Customer thinks they negotiated with one person; they negotiated with three commissioned salespeople in sequence.

**Synthesised math — aftercare-attaching customer:**

| Product | Customer pays | Dealer cost | Dealer gross |
|---|---|---|---|
| Paint protection | $2,000 | ~$500 | $1,500 |
| Window tint | $700 | ~$300 | $400 |
| **Aftercare gross per attached customer (named items only)** | **~$2,700** | | **~$1,900 (≈70% margin)** |

Real aftercare gross per fully-attached customer likely $3-4K (full menu). At 60-80% penetration: average **~$1,500-$3,000 pure aftercare margin per car sold across all customers** — comparable to the entire front-end gross on a Honda.

### Q8 ASKED + ANSWERED 2026-04-29 — Rate markup + F&I commission pool

**The Rate Range Game (the actual mechanic):**
- F&I has an authorized **base rate** they can write down to (e.g. 5%).
- F&I has a **starting rate** they pitch (e.g. 9%).
- They open at the starting rate and watch customer reaction. *"Your repayments are X at 9%"* — see if customer commits.
- If customer balks, they drop. If customer doesn't push, the deal lands at 9%.
- The gap from start-rate to floor-rate is dealer commission, **calibrated per customer to whatever they'll accept** — not a fixed markup.
- Higher rate = more dealer money over the loan term.
- Adrian flagged: *"I don't know specifically what the rate would be — varies given industry standard."*

**The Cash-to-Finance Interrogation Script:**
- F&I assumes "cash" customers are sourcing the cash from a worse loan elsewhere.
- Structured questions: *"Where's the cash from? Savings? Payment from someone? Previous loan? Business money? How much are you paying to use that cash flow?"*
- Goal: identify the underlying capital cost so dealer finance can undercut it.

**The Business Manager's Commission:**
- **10-15% of total F&I gross pool** they generate (not per-product).
- Aligned with maximum extraction across the entire customer (rate + insurance + warranty + GAP + LPI all stacked), not single-product optimization.
- Why the F&I room feels relentless: the manager's income depends on stacking everything.
