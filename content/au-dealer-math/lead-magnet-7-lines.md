## Page 3 — Line 1: "Dealer Delivery"

**What it says:** a separate line item on your contract, usually labelled as the cost to "prepare your vehicle for delivery."

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

This line is mostly dealer margin disguised as cost.

The actual out-of-pocket cost to a dealer to prep a new car — fuel, software update, plastics removed, wash, PDI labour — sat comfortably under $1,000 per car at every dealership I worked at. The number on this line is usually multiple times that.

What I commonly saw quoted:
- Mass-market brands: $1,500 – $3,000+
- Luxury brands: $4,000 – $7,000

That's not the cost of preparing a car. That's profit packaged as preparation.

**The fix:**

Cap your acceptable dealer delivery before you walk in:
- $500 – $1,000 for mass-market
- $1,500 – $2,000 for luxury

Tell the dealer what your cap is. If they say "it's a fixed cost," ask for the breakdown. If they can't or won't show you costs that justify the number, you're looking at margin, not cost. Walk, and call the next dealer — every dealership has a different "fixed" dealer delivery, which means it isn't fixed.

This single line is the most universal money-save in Australian car buying. Every new car you'll ever sign has it. Most people don't even know to look at it.

This is the smallest asymmetry on this contract. The bigger ones are below.

## Page 4 — Line 2: "Trade-In Value: As Inspected"

**What it says:** the figure you signed for is the figure they pay.

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

What you signed isn't always what you get. Dealers can — and do — reassess a trade-in "as inspected" after the deal is locked, dropping the figure on the day of changeover. By then you're emotionally and logistically committed to the new car. The dealership knows it.

The line itself is just the surface mechanic. The actual play happens earlier — before you ever sit down to sign — in a sequence of moves I call **the Walk-Around Doubt-Plant**:

1. The salesperson does a slow inspection of your trade and verbally catalogues flaws (real ones, exaggerated ones, sometimes invented ones).
2. They get a wholesale figure from the manager (the real floor).
3. They quote you well below the floor (the ceiling of the negotiation).
4. They re-engage with "as inspected" wording at sign-time.

Each step has a purpose. Each step is rehearsed.

**The fix:**

Get your trade figure agreed in writing — with the words "as appraised" or "guaranteed" — BEFORE you sign anything on the new car. Better: sell privately. Carsales takes 2–3 weeks but typically nets 15–25% more than dealer trade.

## Page 5 — Line 3: "Finance Through Dealer Panel Rate"

**What it says:** the dealer arranges your finance through their broker network.

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

The rate they offer you isn't the rate the lender charges them. There's a spread. The dealer keeps part of it.

In my time, this spread had a name internally. We called it **the Rate Range Game**: the F&I manager has a base rate from the lender (the floor) and a maximum allowed upcharge (the ceiling). Where they place YOUR rate inside that range depends on one thing — how much you push back.

- Don't ask → you pay near the ceiling.
- Ask once → they meet you somewhere in the middle.
- Walk in with a competing pre-approval and demand they beat it → you get near the floor.

The math is simple. On a $40,000 loan over 5 years, a 1% rate difference is roughly $1,200 in interest. A 2% difference, $2,400. You don't see this on the monthly payment — they show you a number that sounds reasonable. The lifetime cost is what changes.

**The fix:**

Pre-approval at your bank or credit union before you walk in. Get the number. Make the dealer match or beat it in writing. If they can't, the panel wasn't the best deal for you — it was the best deal for them.

## Page 6 — Line 4: "Loan Protection / Optional but Recommended"

**What it says:** GAP insurance, mechanical extended warranty, payment protection — bundled and "optional."

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

The aftercare products on this line — and the four to six others they'll pitch you alongside it — aren't priced at retail. The margins on them are the highest in the dealership.

In my time, I saw aftercare products written up at multiples of dealer cost. Paint protection. Ceramic coating. Fabric protection. Tint. Dash cams. Extended warranty. The dealer-side cost on most of these is a fraction of what you're being charged.

The pitch happens in what I call **The Aftercare Room** — a separate office, often a separate person, separate language, separate timing. By the time you're there, you've signed the metal price. You're tired. You want to drive home. The aftercare person knows this, and the room is designed around it. In the dealerships I worked at, attach rates on at least one product hit 60–80%.

**The fix:**

Decline at the table. Politely, firmly, every product, in order. If you genuinely need one of them, you can buy it outside the dealership at a fraction of the markup — and many of them duplicate cover you already have through your existing comprehensive insurance or the manufacturer warranty.

## Page 7 — Line 5: "Estimated Payout Figure"

(only applies if you have an existing car loan being rolled over)

**What it says:** the amount your existing finance company says is owing on your current car loan.

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

The word *estimated* in this line is doing serious work.

When you trade in a financed car, the dealership pays out your existing loan. The figure on the contract is pulled from your bank's system days before settlement. By the time the actual payout happens, that figure can shift — late fees, daily interest accruals, early-repayment penalties, processing fees. If the actual payout comes back higher than the estimate, the dealership has two options: absorb it or pass it to you. They almost always pass it.

The more dangerous version is when your existing loan is HIGHER than your trade-in's market value. That's negative equity. Dealerships don't avoid customers with negative equity — they specifically target them, because the rollover into a new finance contract is one of the most profitable structures in the industry.

In my experience, a customer rolling negative equity into a new loan typically added $5,000–$15,000+ to the lifetime cost of their next car, hidden inside the monthly repayment.

**The fix:**

Get the actual payout figure from your existing bank in writing the day before signing. Compare it to the trade-in figure. If there's a gap, do NOT sign — close the existing loan separately, then start the new deal fresh.

## Page 8 — Line 6: "Pre-Delivery Inspection: Completed"

**What it says:** the dealership has inspected your vehicle before handover.

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

PDI thoroughness varied dramatically across the dealerships I worked at. Some genuinely inspected every car. Others tick-boxed the form so the salesperson could chase the next deal.

There's no public data on which dealerships do which. There's no rating system. You usually don't get to see the inspection sheet. And by the time a problem surfaces — typically weeks after handover — you're chasing the dealership, not the manufacturer.

**The fix:**

Pay $150–$250 for an independent pre-delivery inspection from a local mechanic. Ask the dealership to allow your inspector access before handover. If they refuse, that tells you something about the inspection itself.

## Page 9 — Line 7: "The Handoff"

**What it says:** this isn't always a printed line on the contract. It's the line of speech the salesperson delivers when they hand you off to the finance manager.

**What I saw on the dealer side:**

This is the most important moment in the entire transaction, and almost no buyer recognises it.

What the salesperson says when they hand you off is calibrated. "Right, let's get you set up with [name], they'll just take care of the paperwork." That phrase has a job: it makes you feel the deal is done, the metal price is locked, and the next step is administrative.

It's not. The next step is the second of three rooms — what I call **the Three-Room Trap**:

1. **Showroom** — metal price (sales)
2. **Finance Manager's Office** — interest rate + finance products + aftercare upsells
3. **Aftercare Desk** — the final pitch + delivery

Each room has a different person, a different objective, a different psychological state they want you in. By the time you've walked through all three, you've spent more than you realised on a deal you thought was done in Room 1.

The handoff phrase is the moment you can still turn it around. Once you walk into Room 2, the choreography takes over.

**The fix:**

- Ask for time between rooms. Step outside. Call your bank. Re-read your offer.
- Decline products on the spot — never "I'll think about it" (that's the language they're trained to convert).
- Refuse to let the F&I manager re-open the metal price under any pretence.
