## [1:30–3:00] WHAT THE AFTERCARE ROOM ACTUALLY SELLS

*Visual: top-down shot of a desk with brochures fanned out — outlines visible but text in soft DOF blur. Brochure outlines suggest paint protection, window tint, dash camera, ceramic coating, fabric protection. Cream paper on dark grey laminate. Subtle Kodak Portra grain.*

> The aftercare manager goes by a few different titles depending on the dealership. Pre-delivery manager. Vehicle accessories manager. Sometimes just the aftercare desk. Title doesn't matter. The job is the same.
>
> Their menu sits in a few categories. Let me walk you through the main ones.
>
> Paint protection. A coating applied to the body of the car, usually claimed to extend the look of the paint and reduce the chance of fade or staining. Common term, often eight to twelve weeks of coverage warranty depending on the product.
>
> Window tint. Film applied to the side and rear glass. Reduces interior heat, blocks UV, gives the car a darker look.
>
> Interior protection. A treatment for the seats and carpet — fabric or leather — meant to make stains easier to lift.
>
> Dash cameras. Front, rear, or dual-channel. Usually wired and hard-mounted by the dealership rather than plug-in.
>
> Ceramic coating. The premium tier of paint protection. Multi-year warranty, harder to apply, higher retail.
>
> Tyre and rim insurance. Pays for kerbed wheel repair or tyre replacement under defined conditions.
>
> Some dealerships add fabric protection, alloy upgrades, sometimes nudge bars and roof racks if you're buying a ute.
>
> That's the menu.
>
> Each one of those products, on its own, looks reasonable. Maybe even sensible depending on your car and your situation. The question isn't whether any single one is worth it. The question is what the math looks like once they're stacked, and what the dealer's actual cost is on the items they're stacking.

## [3:00–5:00] THE MATH REVEAL — MARGINS + PENETRATION

*Visual: split-screen, top-down. Left side: three brochure outlines stacked. Right side: an invoice with three line items highlighted in soft outback orange, totals at the bottom in DOF blur. Mid-frame: hands holding pen above the page, faceless, cropped at chest. On-screen text overlays in DaVinci will burn the dollar figures — script writes them out.*

> Here's the part most buyers don't see.
>
> Take a paint protection plan. Customer-facing retail can sit anywhere from fifteen hundred to two and a half thousand dollars depending on the brand and the dealership. The cost basis to the dealer for the materials and the labour to apply it is, in my experience, somewhere around four to six hundred dollars. That's a margin in the range of seventy to seventy-five percent.
>
> Window tint. Retail typically six hundred to seven hundred dollars on a sedan, more on an SUV. Dealer cost — the film, the tinting bay time — sits around two to three hundred. Margin in the range of fifty to sixty percent.
>
> Stack those two together and you've got a customer-facing total around twenty-seven hundred dollars, with the dealer's gross sitting somewhere near nineteen hundred of that.
>
> Now scale it up. Add the dash cam. Add interior protection. Add ceramic if they walk you up to the premium tier. A fully attached customer can sit at a customer-facing aftercare bill anywhere from three to four thousand dollars, with the dealer's cost basis nowhere near that.
>
> That's the margin stack.
>
> Then there's the second number that matters. The penetration rate. That's the percentage of customers who say yes to at least one aftercare product on their way through the room.
>
> In the dealerships I worked at, aftercare attach was sitting between sixty and eighty percent. That figure varies by brand, by dealership, by aftercare manager. But it's high.
>
> For comparison — finance attach, the room I covered in my last video, sits between twenty and thirty percent.
>
> So aftercare is two to three times more attached than finance. Three out of four customers say yes to something. Margins on the named items sit between sixty and seventy-five percent.
>
> Across the customer population, weighted by who attaches and who doesn't, that adds up to an average aftercare gross sitting somewhere between fifteen hundred and three thousand dollars per car sold. On a Honda-class new-car deal, that's comparable to the front-end gross on the car itself.
>
> The car is one source of margin. The aftercare room is another, and on attach, it's roughly the same size.

## [5:00–7:00] WHY PENETRATION IS SO HIGH — THE STRUCTURAL DESIGN

*Visual: top-down sequence. First shot: customer's hands receiving keys on a lanyard, faceless, cropped at chest. Second shot: same hands now resting on a desk with a brochure being slid across by a second pair of hands. Third shot: pen offered, signature space outback-orange-highlighted.*

> Here's why aftercare attach sits so much higher than finance attach.
>
> By the time you walk into the aftercare manager's office, three things have already happened.
>
> One, you've agreed on a price. The hard part of the negotiation is over, in your mind. Whatever you said yes to in the next room is going to feel small compared to what you just spent on the car itself.
>
> Two, the salesperson has signposted you. They've used a calm, low-friction line to walk you through. Something close to — *what I'll do next, I'll introduce you to the aftercare manager. They'll go through a few items on the vehicle that you might want to add to the car.* That line is doing real work. It frames the next room as an extension of the purchase you've already made, not a second negotiation.
>
> Three, you're tired. Most car deals close after a long Saturday on the floor. Your decision-fatigue threshold has been chipped at all day.
>
> Now you're sitting in front of someone whose only job, full-time, is to walk you through that menu and have you say yes to as many items as possible. Their commission is structured around it.
>
> The aftercare manager isn't an admin clerk. They're a commissioned salesperson, like the one you spent the morning with. They have their own word tracks. Their own bundling pitches. Their own product-specific framing for each category — paint protection gets pitched alongside the showroom photo of an unprotected car at five years. Window tint gets pitched on heat and resale. Interior protection gets pitched against your phone-googled cost of professional detailing. Each product has a story.
>
> And the structural reason their attach rate is so high is what I just described — you've already mentally bought, the salesperson has framed the room as part of the deal, and decision fatigue is doing the rest.
>
> That's not a criticism of the person in the chair. They're doing the job they're paid to do. The structure is what creates the result.
>
> Sixty to eighty percent of buyers walk out the door having added between fifteen hundred and three thousand dollars to the deal in a room they barely registered.

## [7:00–8:30] WHAT TO ACTUALLY DO

*Visual: contract on desk, faceless customer's hand resting beside it. Outback orange highlight on three lines: "Paint Protection," "Window Tint," "Interior Protection." Pen tip pointing to the first line. On-screen text overlay (rendered in DaVinci, not MJ): "EACH PRODUCT — SEPARATE PAGE."*

> Here's what you can do about it.
>
> First move. Decide before you walk in. Not in the room.
>
> Whatever aftercare items you actually want — paint protection, tint, dash cam, whatever — make those decisions at home, with a quote in front of you. Compare the dealer's price against an independent detailer or installer. For paint protection, an independent detail shop will typically come in lower than the dealership's plan, often by a few hundred dollars, sometimes by half. For window tint, the gap can be similar.
>
> The dealership will tell you their plan has warranties or workmanship guarantees the independent doesn't. Sometimes that's true. Often the difference is smaller than the price gap suggests. Either way — that comparison happens at home, with both quotes on the table. Not in the aftercare room with the pen already in your hand.
>
> Second move. When the menu comes out, ask for each product on its own piece of paper. Same rule that applies in the finance room applies here. A two-thousand-dollar product rolled into your weekly repayment is invisible. On its own piece of paper with the retail price, the warranty terms, and the inclusions listed — it's a different conversation.
>
> Third move. Slow down. The aftercare room moves fast because everyone in it benefits from speed. The aftercare manager has another customer behind you. The salesperson has another deal on the floor. The dealership wants the cars released. Saying *I want to take this overnight and let you know in the morning* is allowed. They might tell you the price expires when you walk out. Most of the time, that's a soft pressure tactic. The price you negotiated on the car is locked. The aftercare items can be added later, sometimes weeks later, often at the same price.
>
> If they won't let you add them later, that itself tells you something about the deal.
>
> None of this requires confrontation. You're asking for the information in a format you can evaluate. The dealers are used to customers who don't ask. The customer who asks slowly, and waits for a clear answer, is the one who consistently gets the better outcome.
