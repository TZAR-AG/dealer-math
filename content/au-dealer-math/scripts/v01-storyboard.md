# AU Dealer Math — Video 1 Storyboard + Shot List

## Production approach for V1 (mascot-free)

Mascot deliverable arrives Day 5-7. For V1, visuals lean on:
- **40% bold typographic reveals** (Polymatter-style number animations)
- **35% Storyblocks B-roll** (dealership floors, contracts, handshakes — no faces in focus)
- **20% Midjourney still scenes** (stylised illustrations, abstract environments)
- **5% Kling motion clips** (3-second hero motion shots for hook + key transitions)

When Macca mascot lands at V4-V5, character cutaways become the dominant visual layer. V1-V3 establish the bold-number aesthetic that becomes the channel's signature.

**Brand palette throughout:** charcoal #2B2B2B (background) + outback orange #D17A3D (accent) + cream #F5EFE6 (text-on-dark) + white #FFFFFF (key callouts).

### SCENE 4 — Loan-Term-Extension Trick [2:30-4:30]

**VO:** *"Say you tell the salesperson you can do three hundred a week. Sounds reasonable. The salesperson types numbers into the system. Comes back and says: 'mate, three hundred a week — we can absolutely do that.' Job done. Easy. What you don't see is what happens between you saying three hundred a week and them coming back with yes. Three hundred a week over four years is around sixty-two thousand dollars total. Three hundred a week over seven years is around one hundred and nine thousand. Same weekly payment. Forty-six thousand dollars more out of your pocket over the life of the loan. That's not a typo. The same weekly payment can land you at completely different total costs depending on the loan term — and the dealership chooses the term that suits the dealership. Not you. Here's the kicker. With a longer loan term, you can also afford a more expensive car at the same weekly payment. So instead of a forty-thousand-dollar car at four years, you might end up at a fifty-five-thousand-dollar car at seven years. Same three hundred a week. Different vehicle. Different dealership profit."*

### SCENE 5 — Why the Dealer Plays This Game [4:30-6:00]

**VO:** *"Here's the thing most buyers don't know. The salesperson on the floor isn't the only person making money on your deal. There's another office. Behind the salesperson. Sometimes called the business manager. Sometimes called the finance manager. That's where the contract goes after the metal price is agreed. In that office, the dealership's commission isn't just on the front-end gross — the price you negotiated. It's also on the finance. The bigger the loan. The longer the term. The more the dealership earns. Not from you directly — from a percentage paid by the bank to the dealer for arranging the deal. So when you tell the salesperson 'three hundred a week', you've handed over the steering wheel on the loan term. And the loan term is where the dealership's biggest finance commission lives. This isn't illegal. It's not even hidden — not technically. It's just structured so that the salesperson asking the question doesn't have to think about it. They're trained to ask. The maths happens upstream."*

### SCENE 6 — The Fix [6:00-7:30]

**VO:** *"Here's the fix. Two lines. Memorise them before you walk into a dealership. When the salesperson asks 'what's your weekly budget?' — don't answer. Don't lie. Don't give a range. Just say: 'I'd rather negotiate the drive-away price first. We can talk about finance separately once we agree on the price.' That's it. Two sentences. What happens next is the salesperson loses their grip on the negotiation. They can't anchor on weekly payments because you haven't given them one. They have to play the actual game — the one where you negotiate the price of the car, drive-away, including all on-road costs, before any finance gets discussed. If they push back. If they say 'I just need to know a budget so I can show you the right car' — say 'show me what's in your range, I'll tell you which one I'm interested in, then we'll talk numbers.' If they keep pushing — that's your tell. They're stuck without the weekly-payment hook. That's the moment you know you've already saved yourself thousands."*

## Editing rules

- **First 30 sec:** something visual changes every 1.5-2 sec. No static holds longer than 2 sec.
- **Scenes 2-6:** 3-4 sec average shot length. Never longer than 6 sec without a cut, zoom, or text reveal.
- **No intro animation logo.** Cold-cut from $300/WK reveal direct to VO.
- **Caption file:** Submagic auto-generate, then manually fix any AU vernacular that misrenders (e.g. "drive-away" might be "drive away" — confirm).
- **Burn in subtitles for hook (0:00-0:15) and fix (6:00-7:30)** — these are the highest-retention moments.
- **End on silence.** No "thanks for watching!" type cue. The sign-off is *"I'm Macca. I do this every morning. AU Dealer Math."* and then 15 sec end-screen.
