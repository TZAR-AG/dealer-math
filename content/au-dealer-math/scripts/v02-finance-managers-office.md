## [1:00–2:30] WHAT THE F&I ROOM IS

*Visual: camera moves through door two. Interior shot — top-down of a desk with two chairs opposite each other. A printed loan document sits face-down on the desk. On one side of the desk: a pen, a calculator, a printed rate sheet. On the other side: empty chair. Subtle Kodak Portra grain, natural light through a window.*

> The finance manager goes by a few names depending on the dealership. Business manager. F and I manager, finance and insurance. Sometimes just the business office.
>
> By the time you sit down with them, you've already mentally signed. You agreed on the car. You shook hands. You've probably been offered a coffee. The hard part's over, in your mind.
>
> That's the design.
>
> Here's what I mean by that.
>
> When the salesperson walks you from the floor to the finance office, there's a specific line they use. Not every salesperson says it the same way, but the structure is the same at every dealership I worked at.
>
> It goes something like this. What I'll do next is introduce you to the business manager. They'll talk you through settlement options and a couple of things for the vehicle.
>
> Short. Casual. Low friction. The customer nods and follows.
>
> That line is called the signposting handoff. It exists because without it, a meaningful number of customers get to the threshold of the finance office and baulk. The word finance triggers the part of their brain that's been on guard all day. If they're not pre-framed, some of them walk.
>
> So the salesperson's job, right after you've signed the metal, is to make the next two rooms feel like admin. Not like a second and third negotiation. Which is what they actually are.
>
> Every finance manager I ever worked with was a commissioned salesperson. Not a neutral processor. Not an admin clerk. A salesperson with their own word tracks, their own pressure techniques, and their own income depending on what you say yes to before you leave that room.
>
> The customer thinks they negotiated with one person. They negotiated with three. In sequence.

## [2:30–4:30] THE RATE RANGE GAME

*Visual: split-screen. Left side: a printed rate sheet with a range visible — bottom rate circled at five percent, top rate circled at nine percent. Right side: a loan-term calculator. Orange arrow pointing to the spread between the two circled numbers. On-screen text: "SPREAD = DEALER COMMISSION" — bold charcoal on cream.*

> Here's the mechanic that most people don't know exists.
>
> When you walk into the finance office, the manager already knows what rate the bank has approved you at. The bank communicates an approval. Let's say the bank approved you at five percent.
>
> The finance manager does not tell you that rate.
>
> They open with a different one. Something like nine percent. Sometimes higher. They present your repayments at that rate and they watch.
>
> If you say yes, the deal gets written at nine percent.
>
> If you hesitate or push back, they have room to drop. Maybe to eight, maybe to seven, maybe to six. The floor is wherever the bank's base rate sits. But you only find out the floor exists if you push hard enough to get there.
>
> The gap between where they opened and where they finally wrote the deal, that spread multiplied across the entire loan term, is dealer commission paid by the lender.
>
> To put numbers on it: if you borrow forty thousand dollars over five years and the rate markup is two percentage points, the additional interest cost on your side is roughly two to three thousand dollars over the life of that loan. The finance manager takes a cut of that. The dealership keeps the rest. The bank facilitates it.
>
> I want to be clear. The exact figures vary. I don't know what rate any specific dealership is authorised to write down to. Industry standard varies. What I can tell you, because I saw it, is that the structure is real. The authorized floor exists. The starting pitch is not the floor.
>
> And the key thing: the deal you get depends entirely on how hard you push. Not on some objective fair rate the finance manager arrived at. On your pushback.
>
> The customer who says nine percent sounds fine pays more than the customer who says, what's the lowest rate available.
>
> Same bank. Same approval. Different outcomes.
>
> That's not a conspiracy. That's just the mechanics of a rate range that a lot of people don't know they can push on.

## [4:30–6:00] THE COMMISSION POOL AND WHY THE ROOM FEELS RELENTLESS

*Visual: top-down shot of a desk with multiple product brochures fanned out. Headings visible: "Loan Protection," "Extended Warranty," "GAP Cover," "Insurance." On-screen text burns in: "F&I GROSS POOL." Outback orange arc connects each brochure back to a central point.*

> Here's why the finance office doesn't stop at the rate.
>
> The finance manager's commission isn't structured per product. It's a percentage of the total gross pool they generate from you, across everything you say yes to in that room.
>
> From what I've seen, that's somewhere in the range of ten to fifteen percent of total F and I gross. I might be wrong on the exact figure. It varies by dealership and arrangement. But the structure means their income goes up with every additional product you take.
>
> Loan protection insurance, GAP cover, extended warranty on used cars, combined insurance packages. GAP cover, by the way, is a product that pays the difference between what you owe on the car and what the insurer pays out if the car's written off.
>
> Each product on its own might sound reasonable. The finance manager's job is to make each one sound like the sensible choice for your situation. And their income depends on how many of them you agree to.
>
> That's not a criticism of the person in the chair. It's the structure they're operating inside.
>
> There's one type of customer I want to flag specifically. The cash buyer.
>
> Most cash isn't actually cash. Most people paying cash for a car are drawing from a mortgage redraw or a line of credit. The finance manager's job is to convert them. The pitch usually sounds like, our rate beats your mortgage rate, your car's paid off over a much shorter term. Sometimes that's true. Often the full math, when you run it across fees and the total term, doesn't hold up the way it looks in a quick comparison in that room.
>
> There's also the novated lease customer. That's a separate video. The rate disclosure mechanics on novated leases are worth their own breakdown. I'll get there.

## [6:00–7:30] WHAT TO ACTUALLY DO

*Visual: customer character at the desk. Confident posture. On-screen text overlay: "ASK FOR THE FLOOR RATE." Outback orange underline. Camera stays top-down.*

> Here's what you can do about it.
>
> The first two moves I covered last week still apply here. Bring real bank pre-approval, and time the manufacturer's subsidised fixed-rate campaign if one's running. I won't repeat them in full.
>
> But there's one move specific to this room that I want to add. It's the move that exposes the rate range game in a single question.
>
> When the finance manager presents your repayments, ask this. What rate has the bank approved me at, and what rate is written into this contract.
>
> Two different questions. Two different answers.
>
> Most finance managers, when asked that directly, will either show you the paperwork or confirm there's a difference. Some will tell you the two rates are the same. If they are, the manufacturer is running a flat-fee campaign and you've accidentally timed it right. More often, they're not the same, and now you know the gap exists and you can push on it.
>
> The point is you have to ask. They will not volunteer it.
>
> Second move. When the product stack comes out, loan protection, GAP cover, warranties, ask for each one on its own piece of paper with the retail price and the coverage conditions listed. Not bundled into the repayments. Separate.
>
> A two-thousand-dollar product rolled into sixty monthly payments is invisible. On its own piece of paper it's a different conversation.
>
> None of this requires confrontation. You're asking for the information in a format you can actually evaluate. The dealers are used to customers who don't ask. But the customer who asks quiet, specific questions and doesn't move until they get a clear answer is the one who consistently gets the better deal.
