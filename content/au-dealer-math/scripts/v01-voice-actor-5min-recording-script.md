# AU Dealer Math — Voice Actor 5-Min Recording Script

**Send this to the Fiverr voice actor AFTER they pass the audition line.**

This is the 5-min recording that becomes the input for ElevenLabs Professional Voice Clone training. The cloned voice then narrates every AU Dealer Math video.

---

## Recording brief recap

- **Total length target:** ~5 minutes (~750 words)
- **Pace:** moderate, calm-confident
- **Tone:** confident mate at the pub who happens to have analytical intelligence — NOT corporate radio, NOT bro-podcaster, NOT laid-back
- **Pauses:** half-beat before key dollar figures and emphatic claims
- **Audio:** WAV or 320kbps MP3, 44.1kHz minimum, single take per section preferred
- **Recording requirements:** XLR mic + interface (AT2020/SM7B/RE320 class), quiet treated room
- **Licensing:** full commercial usage including AI cloning rights via ElevenLabs PVC (already confirmed in audition message)

---

## The 5 sample scripts (read continuously, with natural pacing between)

Recommended approach: read each section as a separate take, with a 2-3 second silence between sections so the editor can split cleanly. ElevenLabs PVC trains better on diverse takes.

---

### Section 1 — Calm explainer (~150 words, ~60 sec)

> Most Aussies who walked into a dealership last weekend lost three grand before they sat down. Not because the salesperson lied. Not because the customer was naive. Because the maths is structured to land somewhere specific — and most buyers don't know how to read the back end of the deal.
>
> I spent ten years selling new cars. Started on the floor at a Japanese mass-market brand. Moved to a European luxury brand. Sat in every chair from sales executive to general manager. What I learned is that the price you negotiate isn't the deal. The deal is everything bolted around the price — the trade-in, the finance, the aftercare, the warranty. By the time you've signed all of it, the dealership has earned five times what you thought.
>
> This channel is the one I wish I'd had access to before I walked into my first dealership.

---

### Section 2 — Slightly confrontational insider (~140 words, ~55 sec)

> Here's something the salesperson on the floor genuinely doesn't know.
>
> When the manufacturer ships a car to the dealership, there's a number on the invoice that nobody on the showroom floor sees. It's called wholesale support — sometimes industry insiders call it holdback. It's a rebate from the manufacturer to the dealership, paid quarterly, calculated as a percentage of the sticker.
>
> The salesperson isn't paid on it. So they don't see it. So when they tell you *"I'm losing money on this deal, mate, my manager is going to kill me"* — they actually believe it. That's the trick. It's not deception. It's compartmentalisation. The dealership protects its margin by hiding it from the people you negotiate with.

---

### Section 3 — Numbers + maths read-through (~130 words, ~50 sec)

> Let's run a basic calculation.
>
> Three hundred dollars a week. Over four years, that's around sixty-two thousand four hundred dollars total. Over seven years, same weekly payment, that's about one hundred and nine thousand two hundred dollars total. Forty-six thousand eight hundred dollars more out of your pocket — for the same weekly figure you originally agreed to.
>
> Your dealer doesn't pick the term that suits you. They pick the term that fits their commission structure. The longer the loan, the bigger the finance manager's percentage. So when you give them the weekly number, you've handed them the steering wheel.
>
> The maths isn't complicated. The dealers just hope you don't run it.

---

### Section 4 — AU vernacular range (~140 words, ~55 sec)

> Look, mate — I'm not going to tell you every dealer is a crook. They're not. Most of them are running a business in a tight industry. EOFY pressure. Floor plan interest. Manufacturer targets. Their job is to get the deal done. Yours is to know what you're signing.
>
> Drive-away pricing. On-road costs. Stamp duty. Reg. CTP. Dealer delivery. All those line items have a real number behind them — and a markup baked on top. When the salesperson hands you the offer-to-purchase upside down so you can read it right way up, every line is in play.
>
> Some you can move. Some you can't. The ones the salesperson doesn't tell you about — those are usually the ones with the most room.

---

### Section 5 — Story / emotional beat (~140 words, ~55 sec)

> First deal I ever did, I sold a Honda HRV in Morpheus Blue to a woman called Janet. She was Wednesday night, walk-in, no appointment.
>
> The asking was twenty grand. She offered eighteen. We went back and forth — me and her at the desk, my manager in the office behind the glass, watching. By the end of the night the contract said nineteen-five. Janet drove out feeling like she'd squeezed me. Felt like she'd won.
>
> The dealership had always been willing to do nineteen-five. The whole back-and-forth was theatre. I was twenty years old and I didn't even know it. The car was the delivery mechanism. The feeling of winning was the product.
>
> That's what this channel is about. The bits behind the glass.

---

## Why these 5 sections (training rationale for ElevenLabs PVC)

1. **Section 1** — calm narrative voice (default 70% of channel content)
2. **Section 2** — slight emotional escalation (used in reveal moments, ~15% of content)
3. **Section 3** — number-heavy reading (~10% of content)
4. **Section 4** — AU vernacular density (recurring across all videos)
5. **Section 5** — storytelling tone with first-person scene (Macca's signature mode)

Combined, the 5 sections give ElevenLabs PVC enough range that a single cloned voice can render any future script in Macca's locked tone without artefacts.
