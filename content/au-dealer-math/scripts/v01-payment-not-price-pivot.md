## [1:00–2:30] THE QUESTION + WHY IT WORKS

*Visual: a salesperson cartoon character (will integrate Macca-mascot once Fiverr lands) leaning across a desk toward the buyer character, mouth open mid-question. Stylised speech bubble.*

> Here's the question. It comes early. Sometimes within the first ninety seconds of you walking onto the lot.
>
> *"What's your budget?"*
>
> Or the version that's a little softer:
>
> *"Are you wanting to spread it across, say, weekly payments? What works for you per week?"*
>
> The salesperson sounds helpful. Like they're trying to find a deal that fits. They are — but not the way you think.
>
> The moment you answer that question, the whole conversation has been moved. Off the price of the car. Off the negotiation that happens once. And onto a number that's much easier for the dealership to control: your weekly payment.
>
> Here's why that matters.
>
> Here's the thing. This question — *"what's your weekly budget?"* — isn't something a salesperson came up with on their own. It's trained.
>
> When I started on the floor at the Japanese mass-market brand I worked at, the first document they handed me was called *Road to a Sale*. It's the playbook every new salesperson learns. The question about the weekly budget sits at step three.
>
> Step one is rapport. Step two is needs analysis. Step three is the budget pivot. By the time you've answered, you're already on the dealer's track. The rest of the conversation runs on rails.
>
> Why is it locked in this early? Because once the conversation moves to weekly payments, three other people in the building start running their own numbers on you. The salesperson on the floor is just the front door. You're about to meet two more rooms after you sign the metal — and the math the dealer does to extract from you happens long before you sit down with a pen.
>
> Here's the rest of how this works.

## [2:30–4:30] THE LOAN-TERM-EXTENSION TRICK

*Visual: split-screen comparison. Left side: "$300/week × 4 years = $62,400 total." Right side: "$300/week × 7 years = $109,200 total." Bold yellow numbers. Outback orange arrow pointing to the difference: "$46,800 more."*

> Say you tell the salesperson you can do three hundred a week.
>
> Sounds reasonable. The salesperson types numbers into the system. Comes back and says: *"mate, three hundred a week — we can absolutely do that."*
>
> Job done. Easy.
>
> What you don't see is what happens between you saying *three hundred a week* and them coming back with *yes*.
>
> Three hundred a week over four years is around sixty-two thousand dollars total.
>
> Three hundred a week over seven years is around one hundred and nine thousand.
>
> Same weekly payment. Forty-six thousand dollars more out of your pocket over the life of the loan.
>
> That's not a typo.
>
> The same weekly payment can land you at completely different total costs depending on the loan term — and the dealership chooses the term that suits the dealership. Not you.
>
> Here's the kicker. With a longer loan term, you can also afford a more expensive car at the same weekly payment. So instead of a forty-thousand-dollar car at four years, you might end up at a fifty-five-thousand-dollar car at seven years. Same three hundred a week. Different vehicle. Different dealership profit.
>
> Now here's where it gets sharper at the luxury end of the market.
>
> At the German luxury brand I worked at, customers didn't take a four-year or seven-year loan in the same way. Most of them were sold something called a Guaranteed Future Value plan. It's a structural finance product — manufacturer-backed.
>
> The way it works: the manufacturer guarantees a buyback price at the end of the term. Hundred-thousand-dollar car, three-year term, sixty-thousand-kilometre cap, and the manufacturer guarantees they'll buy it back for around sixty-five thousand at the end. Sounds reasonable.
>
> Here's the catch. The customer's monthly payment over those three years covers depreciation plus interest plus product cost. It does not cover principal-to-ownership. At the end of the term, the customer has three options: hand the car back, buy it out for the guaranteed value, or — most likely — trade up to the next model.
>
> The customer never owns the car. They rent it through finance, then they trade up. Forever. That's not a finance product. That's a recurring-revenue treadmill, and it's why luxury car finance feels like it never ends.

## [4:30–6:00] WHY THE DEALER PLAYS THIS GAME

*Visual: cutaway to a back office. Three doors, three desks. Each labelled. Sales · Aftercare · Finance. Camera pans through.*

> Here's the thing most buyers don't know.
>
> The salesperson on the floor isn't the only person making money on your deal. There's another office. Behind the salesperson. Sometimes called the business manager. Sometimes called the finance manager.
>
> That's where the contract goes after the metal price is agreed.
>
> In that office, the dealership's commission isn't just on the front-end gross — the price you negotiated. It's also on the *finance*.
>
> The bigger the loan. The longer the term. The more the dealership earns. Not from you directly — from a percentage paid by the bank to the dealer for arranging the deal.
>
> So when you tell the salesperson *"three hundred a week"*, you've handed over the steering wheel on the loan term. And the loan term is where the dealership's biggest finance commission lives.
>
> This isn't illegal. It's not even hidden — not technically. It's just structured so that the salesperson asking the question doesn't have to think about it. They're trained to ask. The maths happens upstream.
>
> But here's what most buyers genuinely don't know. There isn't one office behind the salesperson. There are two.
>
> After the metal price gets agreed, the customer doesn't go straight to finance. They go to the aftercare manager first. Paint protection, window tint, dash cameras, interior protection — these get packaged together. The retail on the package can sit anywhere from fifteen hundred to three thousand dollars depending on the dealership and how it gets bundled. The dealer's cost on the whole package is up to five hundred. That's a one-thousand to twenty-five-hundred-dollar gross on a single attach, and the industry's standard attach rate is sixty to eighty per cent. The aftercare manager's job is to package it up in the way that pulls the most margin per customer — framed to the buyer as the most value, framed to the dealership as the most gross.
>
> Then they go to the finance manager. That's where the loan gets written and the rate gets marked up. The bank approves the customer at one rate; the contract gets written at a higher one. The spread, multiplied across the loan term, is dealer commission.
>
> And there's a layer the salesperson on the floor can't see, called the holdback. Even when the dealer tells the customer *"we can't go any lower"* — there's still a margin paid by the manufacturer at the end of the quarter. Plus volume rebates that scale with how much wholesale stock the dealership takes on. The exact figures vary by brand and quarter, but the structure is universal.
>
> The customer thinks they're negotiating with one person. They're negotiating against three commissioned salespeople in sequence — and the dealership's biggest revenue lines aren't even on the table.

## [6:00–7:30] THE FIX — ACTIONABLE CONSUMER TAKEAWAY

*Visual: customer character standing taller, confident posture. On-screen text overlays the two scripts as Macca speaks them.*

> Here's the fix. Two lines. Memorise them before you walk into a dealership.
>
> When the salesperson asks *"what's your weekly budget?"* — don't answer. Don't lie. Don't give a range.
>
> Just say:
>
> *"I'd rather negotiate the drive-away price first. We can talk about finance separately once we agree on the price."*
>
> That's it. Two sentences.
>
> What happens next is the salesperson loses their grip on the negotiation. They can't anchor on weekly payments because you haven't given them one. They have to play the actual game — the one where you negotiate the price of the car, drive-away, including all on-road costs, before any finance gets discussed.
>
> If they push back. If they say *"I just need to know a budget so I can show you the right car"* — say *"show me what's in your range, I'll tell you which one I'm interested in, then we'll talk numbers."*
>
> If they keep pushing — that's your tell. They're stuck without the weekly-payment hook. That's the moment you know you've already saved yourself thousands.
>
> Two more moves before you walk in.
>
> First, if you're financing — bring real bank pre-approval. Not an estimate. A signed letter from your bank with the rate, the term, and the total cost of the loan in writing. When the finance manager opens with their rate, hand them the letter. Make them beat the total cost of the loan, including all fees, on paper. Their first move will be to point at a small fee on your bank's letter and say *"the bank charges that, we don't."* Hold the line. Total cost over the full term, all fees included, both papers side by side. That's the only fair comparison.
>
> Second, time it. A few times a year, the manufacturer runs a subsidised low-rate finance campaign. The exact rate varies by brand and quarter — but when one of these campaigns is on, the dealer makes a small flat fee instead of a rate spread. Their incentive flips from extracting margin to closing the volume bonus. That's the cleanest finance deal a dealership will ever offer you. If a campaign is running and you're ready to buy, that's the window.
>
> Get the price first. Bring the bank letter. Time the rate campaign. Three moves. None of them require any maths the dealer didn't already do for you.
